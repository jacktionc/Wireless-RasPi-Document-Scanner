# Wireless-RaspberryPi-Document-Scanner
## Introduction: 

Since society first began, people have always needed to store information. This came in the form of oral traditions, wall carvings, and manuscripts. Now, in the digital age, the need to efficiently store, organize, and reference information has become extremely prevalent to our day to day lives, especially in academia. University students will often create and handle many different physical documents, including but not limited to, essays, lecture notes, assignments, scratch work, and exams. As their academic career continues, this physical information can become cumbersome and difficult to organize, especially for CS students who oftentimes must separately manage their digital as well as physical document folders. 

To address these challenges and improve ease of file access, a simple solution is to combine these folders into one. Our project presents a Raspberry Pi-based document scanner designed to facilitate a quick and easy method for digitizing physical documents. Our project utilizes a Raspberry Pi 4, a Picamera 2 module, and a computer, such that we can provide a compact and efficient solution for the capture and storage of printed and handwritten documents. These digital copies can then be easily accessed and shared, providing a practical way for students to maintain digital backups of their physical work. It also safeguards against accidental loss or damage of documents 

## Problem Characterization:

The main problem we hope to solve with this project is mitigating the physical toll and mental strain that comes with organizing and storing documents in multiple mediums, as well as the risks that arise from vulnerable document mediums. Students often encounter difficulties when managing hard copies of their documents. These difficulties include:
Loss or Damage: Printed and handwritten material is susceptible to damage from environmental factors and improper long-term storage. They are also at risk of being misplaced, all resulting in the loss of valuable information.
Lack of Accessibility: Large volumes of paper can become cumbersome to store, given that the physical storage needed must be increased over time. Eventually, this storage method becomes impractical to move efficiently and time-consuming to backup or pull from. 
Sharing Challenges: In collaborative environments (academic and professional alike), sharing hard copies of documents can become inconvenient and time-consuming compared to their digital counterparts. 

Our document scanner can address these problems by providing a simple and portable method for convenient storage and management of documents. The portable nature of the Pi allows for quick scanning on busy days that don't provide ample time for document organization. Its software manages image capture, preview, and storage along with the ability to synthesize multiple images into one PDF to facilitate better organization and presentation. This focus on accessibility allows for a seamless integration into a student’s workflow and day to day obligations.

### Interfacing with the Pi Camera:
In our application the capture.py module encapsulates all interactions between software, and the onboard Pi Camera. To begin, this section will describe the connection and set-up process of the Pi Camera. The rest will detail the implementation of capture.py’s two member functions “quick_capture()” and “capture_still”, which together encompass the most critical pieces of our application.

### Connection and Setup:
Before the Raspberry Pi can begin interfacing with the camera module, the camera’s ribbon cable must be inserted into the Pi’s camera serial interface slot. A CSI connection facilitates a high speed, low latency image data transfer path to the processor without passing through the USB controller (1). This enables the most essential feature for a modern camera, real time video and image processing. 
Our choice of operating system, Raspberry Pi OS Bookworm, comes with the necessary dependencies for the Pi Camera pre-configured. Therefore after physically connecting the camera, we were immediately able to experiment taking images via the command line.

### capture_still() Implementation:
We began this core of our project by writing a simple Python script using the PiCamera2 library (2) to capture a still image from the Pi camera and save it as a JPEG. As we continued developing other aspects of our application, this eventually evolved into the version of capture_still() we concluded with.
Due to the inherent characteristics of the Pi Camera and its drivers, multiple camera instances cannot be created at once, and thus cannot tolerate concurrent operations (1). This is why all camera operations, including capture_still() are guarded by a “with threading.Lock()” block that guarantees exclusive access. Inside this block is where crucial camera interactions take place. To initiate an operation, a PiCamera2 object is created. This instance is then configured to capture still with a desired size, and image format using the “create_still_configuration()” method. We chose a resolution of 2480x3508 to ensure the aspect ratio is suitable for documents, and a format of RGB888. This configuration is then applied to the camera instance using the “configure()” method. Next, the camera is started using the “start()” method and begins a warmup. The warmup is necessary because the Pi Camera 2 is equipped with features like auto-focus and auto-white-balance (2). To work properly these features require a slight delay from when the camera starts, to when it captures a still. To facilitate this our program simply uses “time.sleep()” for 1 second after starting up the camera module. After the warm up is concluded, the Pi Camera is ready to capture a still image. We do this by calling picamera2’s “capture_array()” function, which creates a numpy array from the latest frame. Immediately following this, we call “stop()” and “close()” on the camera instance to free the camera interface for other aspects of our application.
Once the camera operation is concluded, we begin preparing the image data for later uses. First, we call “ascontiguousarray()” on the numpy array created by “capture_array()”, which ensures the array is contiguous, meaning it's stored as a continuous block of memory. This is to guarantee compatibility with the following image processing methods from the Pillow Library (3). First we convert the image array into a Pillow Image object using “Image.fromarray()”. Next, we convert the RGB image to grayscale using “.convert()” in order to reduce its file size for later file transfers. To complete the capture_still method, we save the final product as a JPEG file using Pillow’s “save()” method.

### quick_capture Implementation:
The need for an alternative capture method came when we were developing the viewfinder component of our application. It demanded a faster, more efficient capture method to maintain a respectable frame rate. The implementation is a variant of “capture_still()” with a few minor tweaks to make it more well suited in supporting what is essentially a live video feed. These tweaks mainly targeted the optimization of the viewfinder, and finding a comfortable medium between quality and latency.
The first of the tweaks was altering the configuration of the picamera2 instance. We chose to use a smaller capture resolution of 620x877 to reduce file sizes as much as possible, while still maintaining a consistent aspect ratio. This made a drastic difference in the latency/framerate of our viewfinder implementation, because of the number of data transfer operations. Another adaptation that reduced latency tremendously was to remove the warmup procedure. We deemed the warm up procedure unnecessary, because the viewfinder is intended only to assist users with lining up their document to be scanned and the quality would still be present in the final still. Our final tweak was to return a “BytesIO” buffer instead of unnecessarily saving each frame of the viewfinder as a JPEG.

### Flask server API:
In order to facilitate data transfers between the Raspberry Pi and the machine running the client, we had to develop a server program. We ended up with a minimal server implementation using the Flask library (4) to enable various HTTP endpoints.
Underneath Flask’s abstraction, is a Web Server Gateway Interface, WSGI, program (5). To begin creating the server we instantiated a “Flask()” object with the name of our application, with the shortcut “Flask(__name__)”. This object is callable and contains vital information for the server. Our Flask application defines three endpoints that respond to HTTP requests from the features in our application.

### Endpoints:
The three endpoints we have are “/ (root)”, “/capture”, and “/preview”. The first of which, the root endpoint, defines a simple health check. Its purpose is to alert the user as to whether or not their Pi is connected to the application, at a glance. It simply returns a connection message with a status code of 200.
The second endpoint “/capture” is intended to respond to requests for a still image capture. It achieves this by accepting and processing “get” requests through HTTP. Inside the implementation we define a file-path/destination in which to save the resulting JPEG, then we try to capture the still using our “capture_still()” method discussed earlier. If either the capturing or file storing processes fail, the endpoint gracefully outputs an error along with a 500 status code. If everything is successful, the “send_file()” function is used to create the response object passed to the client.
The final endpoint “/preview” enables the viewfinder feature, allowing for easy document alignment. This also accepts a HTTP “get” requests, and begins by attempting to capture a preview frame using “quick_capture()”. If the frame capture was unsuccessful, it gracefully throws an error and 500 status code. If it was successful, the response object is instantiated with the raw bytes of the frame using the “make_response()” method. Finally the response headers are updated appropriately to disable caching.

### Client:
Our client program is intended to run on a separate machine from the Pi, and provides an easy to use interface for the end user. We used two primary libraries for this, Tkinter (6) to handle the GUI, and Requests (7) to handle HTTP traffic to our Flask API.

### GUI:
When a user launches the client, they will first be presented by an interface powered by Tkinter. We started building the GUI by first creating the main container for the entire interface, which is done by defining the program class as a subclass of `tk.Tk` (6). We then set the value of various class variables using Tkinter methods like “title()”, “geometry()”, and calling custom methods we created to load the other GUI components. “build_widgets()” is the driving force behind the GUI. This is where we created a container for the status bar to show the connection message from our root endpoint, a subcontainer for the viewfinder that accommodates the stream, and various buttons to control the application. 
Server Health Check:
As previously stated the status bar sends a request to the root endpoint of our API, and displays its response. The “ping_pi()” function does this by leveraging the `get()` function of the Requests library (7) to send an HTTP GET request to the server running on the Pi. If everything is successful it will convert and display the JSON response sent by the server, confirming the Pi is connected to the client. 

### Stream/Viewfinder:
The stream functionality is controlled with three functions “start_stream()”, “stop_stream()”, and “stream_preview()”. The former two functions simply set the value of a boolean class variable “streaming” to control whether the stream is running or paused. The main function that processes the stream, `stream_preview()`, uses Requests’ `get()` function (7) to grab the JPEG bytes from the “/preview” endpoint. If the bytes are successfully received they are converted to a Pillow “Image()” object, and processed/updated into the Tkinter viewfinder container. All of this is done every ~50ms to provide a video-esque output, dependent on the network speed.

### Capture Image:
First, the capture method will suspend the stream in order to gain camera access. It will then send a get request to the “/capture” endpoint, and expects to receive a JPEG image. If it receives the desired response it will first store the response image in an array called pages, discussed later. Afterwards, the image is processed and displayed in the Viewfinders Tkinter component as a thumbnail for the user to verify their capture. Once this process is completed, the stream is restarted using “start_stream()”.

### Saving the Results:
We implemented two ways to save the final file to the client computer. The first being a very simple “download” button, that calls the “download()” function. This saves the most recently captured JPEG to the client machine using the Tkinter function `asksaveasfilename()` (6). Finally, the function “save_pdf()” utilizes the pages array to enable the creation of multi-page PDFs with multiple captures. 

## Conclusion:
In this project, we successfully designed and implemented a portable, Raspberry Pi-based document scanner to address a common but often overlooked problem: The need for efficient and reliable digitization of physical documents. This need is relevant for students and knowledge workers who must manage a growing collection of handwritten notes, printed assignments, and other paper-based materials. Our goal was to make sure the process of scanning and storing documents was more convenient and accessible-transforming a traditionally manual and time-consuming task into a seamless part of a user’s daily workflow.
	The solution we developed uses a Raspberry Pi 4, a PiCamera2 module, and client-server architecture powered by Flask and Tkinter. This combination allowed us to bridge the gap between hardware-level image capture and a user-friendly desktop interface.  By creating an API to handle requests and responses between the Raspberry Pi and a remote client, we also enabled a real-time image preview, capture, and file management over a local network. We implemented careful control to manage access to the camera, handle latency issues by optimizing image resolution and eliminating warm-up time where possible, which ensured the image captures could be quickly saved as JPEGs or compiled into PDFS for long-term storage.
	One major takeaway from the project was the importance of balancing performance with usability. For example, we had to consider both the limitations of the PiCamera and the expectations of the end user when designing the preview stream and still capture functions. We also gained hands-on experience with threading and synchronization, working with low-level hardware connections and building full-stack applications where both frontend and backend are tailored for lightweight systems.
	Moreover, this project reinforced the value of modular design. Each component, whether it was the image processor, the server endpoints, or the GUI, was developed to be independently testable and upgradable. This opens the door to future enhancements, such as adding optical character recognition(OCR), cloud backup, or support for formats beyond standard PDF documents.
	Overall, this project has been a practical exploration into embedded systems, computer vision, and client-server communication. It not only addressed the initial problem of physical document overload but also created a scalable foundation that can be adapted for broader use cases in education, business, and even mobile fieldwork. Our implementation demonstrates that with a modest hardware setup and thoughtful software engineering, meaningful and useful systems can be built to solve everyday problems.
